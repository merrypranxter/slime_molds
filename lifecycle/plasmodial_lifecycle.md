# Plasmodial Lifecycle (Myxomycetes)

The plasmodial slime mold lifecycle is a **shapeshifting drama** spanning microscopic cells to macroscopic blobs to intricate fruiting bodies. This is the main visual spectacle group.

---

## **Lifecycle Stages Overview**
SPORE
↓ (moisture + food)
MYXAMOEBA or SWARM CELL
↓ (cell fusion)
ZYGOTE
↓ (nuclear division without cell division)
PLASMODIUM (multinucleate blob)
↓ (starvation or environmental trigger)
SCLEROTIUM (optional hardened resting stage)
↓ (favorable conditions return)
PLASMODIUM (resumes activity)
↓ (fruiting initiation)
PRIMORDIA
↓ (differentiation)
FRUITING BODIES (sporangia / aethalia / pseudoaethalia / plasmodiocarps)
↓ (spore release)
SPORE (cycle repeats)

---

## **Stage 1: Spore**

**Size:** 5–20 μm (microscopic)

**Visual traits:**
- Round to oval
- Often ornamented (spines, warts, reticulation, ridges)
- Colors: black, brown, purple, yellow, white
- Dust-like when released in mass

**Art potential:**
- Dust clouds
- Microscopic jewel textures
- Atmospheric particle halos
- Spore print patterns

**Duration:** Dormant for years if conditions unfavorable

---

## **Stage 2: Myxamoeba / Swarm Cell**

**Size:** 10–30 μm (microscopic, single-celled)

**Visual traits:**
- Amoeba-like blob (myxamoeba) OR
- Flagellated swimmer (swarm cell)
- Can convert between forms depending on moisture
- Feeds on bacteria, yeast, fungal spores

**Art potential:**
- Amoeboid streaming at micro-scale
- Flagellar propulsion
- Bacterial hunting animations
- Cell fusion rituals

**Duration:** Hours to days

---

## **Stage 3: Zygote**

**Size:** ~20 μm (microscopic)

**Visual traits:**
- Result of myxamoeba/swarm cell fusion (sexual reproduction)
- Single cell with two nuclei (diploid)
- Begins nuclear division WITHOUT cell division

**Art potential:**
- Cell fusion moment
- Nuclear doubling visualization
- Transition to multinucleate state

**Duration:** Brief (transitions rapidly to plasmodium)

---

## **Stage 4: Plasmodium** ⭐ **ICONIC STAGE**

**Size:** Microscopic to 1+ meter

**Visual traits:**
- Multinucleate single-celled blob (thousands to millions of nuclei, no cell walls)
- Visible to naked eye, often brightly colored
- Vein-like network or fan-shaped feeding front
- **Cytoplasmic streaming:** pulsing rhythmic flow through veins (visible in timelapse)
- Colors: yellow, white, orange, pink, purple, grey
- Wetness: mucus-slick, gel-like, translucent edges

**Subtypes:**
- **Protoplasmodium:** Microscopic, thin
- **Aphanoplasmodium:** Small, inconspicuous
- **Phaneroplasmodium:** LARGE, conspicuous, vein-networked (the famous one)

**Behavior:**
- Migrates toward food, away from light (usually)
- Solves mazes (chemotaxis + optimal routing)
- Can fuse with other plasmodia of same species
- Engulfs bacteria, fungal spores, decaying organic matter

**Art potential:**
- **VEIN NETWORKS:** Subway map logic, graph theory, adaptive routing
- **PULSING FLOW:** Peristaltic waves, cytoplasmic tides
- **FEEDING FRONTS:** Amoeboid expansion, exploratory tendrils
- **SLICK WETNESS:** Translucent gel, subsurface glow
- **ALIEN INTELLIGENCE:** Non-neural computation, distributed decision-making

**Duration:** Days to weeks (can be sustained indefinitely with food)

**Famous example:** *Physarum polycephalum* — yellow vein networks, maze-solver, model organism

---

## **Stage 5: Sclerotium** (Optional Resting Stage)

**Size:** Matches plasmodium size before hardening

**Visual traits:**
- Hardened, dried crust of plasmodium
- Colors: dark brown, black, purple, grey
- Cracked surface texture
- Waxy or papery finish

**Trigger:** Starvation, drought, temperature stress

**Behavior:**
- Dormant (can survive months to years)
- Revives as plasmodium when moisture/food returns

**Art potential:**
- Cracked rind aesthetics
- Dormancy/revival rituals
- Waxy crust textures
- Survival architecture

**Duration:** Weeks to years

---

## **Stage 6: Primordia**

**Size:** 0.5–5 mm (visible but tiny)

**Visual traits:**
- Small bumps/knobs forming on plasmodium surface
- Initial differentiation into fruiting structures
- Often translucent or pale
- Clustered or scattered

**Trigger:** Starvation, light exposure, temperature shift

**Art potential:**
- Eruption moment
- Surface budding
- Initiation of complexity

**Duration:** Hours to days

---

## **Stage 7: Fruiting Bodies** ⭐ **ICONIC STAGE**

**Size:** 0.5 mm to several cm

**Visual traits:** Highly variable — this is where morphological diversity EXPLODES

### **Fruiting Body Types:**

#### **A. Sporangium (singular) / Sporangia (plural)**
- Individual stalked or sessile structures
- Most common type
- Parts:
  - **Stipe (stalk):** May be present or absent
  - **Peridium (outer wall):** Can be smooth, warty, netted, iridescent, lime-encrusted
  - **Capillitium (internal threads):** Elastic fibers that help disperse spores
  - **Columella (central axis):** May extend into sporangium
  - **Spore mass:** Color defines the species often (black, purple, yellow, white, rust)

**Examples:**
- *Arcyria* — Red coral tufts with expanding capillitium
- *Stemonitis* — Black feathery pins on dark stalks
- *Cribraria* — Bead towers with netted cages

#### **B. Aethalium (singular) / Aethalia (plural)**
- Massive cushion or pillow
- Formed when entire plasmodium converts into one structure
- No individual sporangia visible
- Often foamy, crusty, or waxy

**Examples:**
- *Fuligo septica* — Sulfur yellow foam pillows
- *Lycogala epidendrum* — Pink-to-brown puffballs
- *Reticularia lycoperdon* — Silver-brown cushions

#### **C. Pseudoaethalium**
- Looks like an aethalium but is actually tightly clustered sporangia
- Individual sporangia visible under magnification

**Examples:**
- *Tubifera ferruginosa* — Raspberry-like rust clusters

#### **D. Plasmodiocarp**
- Plasmodium hardens in place along its vein network
- Retains plasmodium's original shape
- Looks like fossilized veins

**Examples:**
- *Hemitrichia serpula* — Serpentine yellow networks
- *Physarum compressum* — Flattened vein casts

**Art potential:**
- **CORAL ANALOGS:** Trichiales species (Arcyria, Trichia)
- **GOTHIC TOWERS:** Stemonitidales (Stemonitis, Lamproderma)
- **FOAM ARCHITECTURE:** Physarales aethalia (Fuligo)
- **BEAD JEWELRY:** Liceales (Cribraria)
- **LACE STRUCTURES:** Cribraria cages, Arcyria capillitium
- **VEIN FOSSILS:** Plasmodiocarps

**Duration:** Days to weeks (then dries and releases spores)

---

## **Stage 8: Spore Release**

**Mechanism:**
- Peridium breaks down or opens
- Capillitium expands (hygroscopic — responds to humidity)
- Spores puff out as dry dust

**Visual traits:**
- Dust clouds
- Powder bursts
- Atmospheric particle dispersal

**Art potential:**
- Spore ash atmospherics
- Dust release animations
- Wind dispersal logic

## **Notes**

- The plasmodium and fruiting body stages are the most visually dramatic and art-relevant
- Plasmodium stage is where the "intelligence" behavior happens (maze-solving, routing optimization)
- Fruiting bodies are where morphological diversity explodes (hundreds of distinct forms)
- Lifecycle can be disrupted/looped at sclerotium stage indefinitely
- Some species skip sexual reproduction and produce spores directly from plasmodium
